# Draft LinkedIn Promotional Post

## Purpose
Create short, curiosity-driven LinkedIn posts that promote blog articles using a conversational, relatable style.

## Style Guidelines

### Tone & Voice
- **Conversational and personal** - Write like you're sharing a discovery with peers, not teaching a lesson
- **Relatable** - Start with personal experience or admission ("I've been doing X for years...")
- **Curiosity-driven** - Open with an intriguing question or surprising statement
- **Direct and concise** - No fluff, get to the point quickly
- **Authentic** - Share genuine insights and realizations
- **Value-focused** - Lead with bold claims about outcomes, not process details

### Structure Patterns

#### Pattern A: Discovery Style (Question-driven)
1. **Hook** - Question or surprising statement that creates curiosity
   - "Ever heard of a [technique/concept]?"
   - "Did anyone else learn to [practice]?"
   
2. **Clarification** - Brief explanation that it's not what people might think
   - "No, it's not some obscure [misconception]..."
   - "No, I'm not making this up..."

3. **Personal Connection** - Share your experience or realization
   - "I've been [doing X] for years, and I recently realized..."
   - "It was a practice I was taught as a [role]..."

4. **The Insight** - The key realization or problem
   - "Sometimes that [thing] I did? It was actually the right call. But I only knew that looking back."
   - "And frequently in the middle of my explanation, I'd figure the problem out!"

5. **The Bridge** - Connect to the solution
   - "So how do you get better at [problem]?"

6. **The Reveal** - Name the technique (but NOT how to do it)
   - "Turns out, there's a technique for that - It's called [Name]."

7. **Call to Action** - Invite them to learn HOW
   - "I wrote about how to [implement/apply] this [context]: [URL]"

8. **Hashtags** - 2-4 relevant hashtags

#### Pattern B: Direct Value Style (Outcome-driven)
1. **Bold Claim** - Lead with the outcome or counterintuitive insight
   - "The most [superlative] won't [expected action]. They'll be [actual outcome]."
   - "[Role] success isn't about [common belief]. It's about [real driver]."

2. **The Requirement** - What capability this demands
   - "That means [specific skill/action], which takes [human quality]."

3. **The Challenge** - Frame the problem as a question
   - "But how do you [teach/develop/build] [that quality]?"

4. **The Solution Name** - Name the technique with minimal explanation
   - "Teach them the [Technique Name]."
   - "It's [one-line description of what it is]."

5. **Call to Action** - Invite them to learn the implementation
   - "I wrote about how to implement this practice: [URL]"

6. **Hashtags** - 2-4 relevant hashtags

### Length
- Target: 100-150 words
- Keep it scannable and quick to read
- Short paragraphs (1-3 sentences each)
- Use line breaks for readability

### What to Avoid
- **Don't reveal HOW to do the technique** - Only name it and point to its value
- **Don't give step-by-step instructions** - Save that for the blog post
- Don't use formal or academic language
- Don't start with generic phrases like "In today's world..."
- Don't make it sound like a lecture or tutorial
- Don't include emojis unless specifically requested
- **Critical: The CTA should invite readers to learn HOW, not explain the full technique**

## Example Posts

### Example 1: Drucker Memo Post
```
Ever heard of a "Drucker Memo"?

No, it's not some obscure management jargon. It's actually a simple technique your boss (or your boss's boss) might be using to sharpen their judgment.

Here's the thing: I've been writing code for years, and I recently realized something. Sometimes that "temporary hack" I wrote? It was actually the right call. But I only knew that looking back.

So how do you get better at making those calls in the moment?

Turns out, there's a technique for that - It's called the Drucker Memo.

I wrote about how to apply this executive practice to your architecture decisions: https://daniel.scheufler.tech/blog/drucker-memo-architecture/

```

## Usage Instructions

When asked to create a LinkedIn promotional post:

1. **Read the blog article** to understand:
   - Core idea/technique
   - Target audience
   - Key insight or problem it solves
   - The "aha moment" or realization

2. **Identify the hook**:
   - What's surprising or counterintuitive?
   - What executive/senior practice can engineers learn from?
   - What common experience can you reference?

3. **Draft the post** following the structure pattern above

4. **Keep it personal**:
   - Use "I" statements
   - Share genuine realizations
   - Make it relatable to the reader's experience

5. **End with clarity**:
   - Name the technique
   - Provide the link
   - Add relevant hashtags

## Key Principles

- **Curiosity over explanation** - Make them want to click, don't give away everything
- **Value over process** - Show the outcome, invite them to learn the how
- **Name the technique, not the steps** - "It's called X" not "Here's how to do X"
- **Personal over professional** - Share discoveries, not lectures
- **Simple over sophisticated** - Clear language beats clever wordplay
- **Relatable over impressive** - Connect with shared experiences
- **Bridge executive practices to engineering** - Show how "boss-level" techniques apply to code
- **The blog post teaches HOW** - The promo only teaches WHAT and WHY
